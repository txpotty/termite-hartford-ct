Termites foraging for food discover the bait, which contains an insect growth regulator or other slow-acting toxicant. Mud Tubes on Walls or Foundation

Subterranean termites, the most common type in our area, build mud tubes as protected pathways from their underground colonies to their food source – the wood in your home. These tubes, often the width of a pencil, can be found on exterior walls, the foundation, or even inside your basement or crawl space. Finding mud tubes is a strong indicator of a subterranean termite infestation and requires immediate professional attention. These tubes are essentially highways for termites, shielding them from predators and dehydration as they travel between their nest and your house. The presence of even a few small tubes should raise a red flag. They are typically made of soil, saliva, and wood particles, creating a hardened tunnel that allows termites to move freely and safely. Inspecting areas where the foundation meets the ground or where porch pillars connect to the ground is a good starting point for finding these tubes. They can be easily overlooked if you’re not actively looking for them, so conducting regular inspections of your property is highly recommended. Don’t attempt to simply brush away or break the tubes without further action; this will only cause the termites to rebuild them elsewhere and continue their destructive work. Breaking the tubes doesn’t eliminate the colony; it just forces them to move to a different access point. Discarded Termite Wings

Termites swarm, typically in the spring, to reproduce and establish new colonies. After swarming, they shed their wings. Piles of discarded wings, particularly near windows, doors, or other light sources, are a common sign of a recent termite swarm. This indicates that termites have successfully reproduced and are actively attempting to start new infestations. Finding discarded wings is a sure sign that a termite colony is present in or around your home and has reached maturity. These small, translucent wings are fragile and easily break off after swarming. Don’t confuse them with ant wings, which are different in shape and size. Termite wings are typically equal in length, while ant wings are not. Swarming often occurs on warm, humid days after rain. While a swarm is a temporary event, the presence of discarded wings is a lasting indicator of a nearby colony. It signifies that the colony has grown large enough to send out reproductives. Ignoring discarded wings is a mistake; it’s a clear warning sign that a termite infestation is either present or imminent. Finding them indoors suggests that the termite colony is located within your home’s structure or very close by. Finding discarded wings outdoors near your foundation can indicate a colony is present in the soil nearby and attempting to infest your home.
